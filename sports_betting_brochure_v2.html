About This Research

This brochure summarizes a student research project examining how the rapid expansion of sports betting advertising is affecting mental health and financial stability in Whatcom County and beyond.

"Each person with a gambling problem negatively affects an average of six other people — meaning this is a community issue, not just an individual one."

The research draws on peer-reviewed academic sources and a 36-person community survey conducted at Whatcom Community College in Spring 2026.

Research Methods
  • Systematic review of published academic literature (2023–2025)
  • Community survey — 36 WCC respondents, March 2026
  • Analysis of U.S. household financial transaction data
  • Clinical study data from gambling disorder patients

"75% of local respondents felt sports betting advertising makes gambling seem normal or low-risk — the highest consensus finding in our survey."

Since the Supreme Court's 2018 ruling, 39 states have legalized sports betting. Total bets placed have grown 30-fold — reaching $148 billion in 2024, with 94% placed via mobile devices.

"Rapid technological change over the last 20 years has increased accessibility to gambling. Advertising has mirrored this trend, increasing in frequency and complexity." — McGrane et al., 2025

The "Gamblification" of Daily Life

Researchers describe a process of gamblification — the deep embedding of gambling culture into sports and everyday life. Betting operators sponsor teams, advertise during games, and use apps that enable betting anywhere, anytime.

Who Is Most at Risk
  • Young adults ages 18–34
  • Financially constrained households
  • People with high advertising exposure
  • Those with existing anxiety or mood disorders

What Research Shows
Financial Harm

A 2024 NBER study of 230,000+ U.S. households found that following legalization, household savings dropped, credit card debt increased, and overdraft frequency rose — concentrated in financially constrained households. Every dollar bet reduced investment contributions by roughly 99 cents.

"Financial harms include betting more than they can afford (22%), chasing losses (27%), and living in households that cannot pay bills (34%) or rent on time (28%)." — Hing et al., 2023

Mental Health Harm

A systematic review found ~37.9% of problem gamblers have a comorbid mood disorder and ~37.4% have an anxiety disorder. Gambling disorder can precede and cause these conditions — not merely co-occur with them.

Advertising Drives Both

Research confirms a dose-response effect: more ad exposure leads to stronger effects on gambling behavior. Advertising is structurally designed to mislead consumers into believing gambling is skill-based.
