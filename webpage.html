About This Page

This webpage is designed for community members, local organizations, and policymakers in Whatcom County — particularly the Evergreen Council on Problem Gambling (ECPG) and the Opportunity Council of Whatcom County. The goal is to present research findings in a format that is accessible without being oversimplified, and actionable without being alarmist. The tone is intentionally calm and informational — this is a public health issue, not a scare campaign. The page follows the structure of the underlying completion report, moving from background context through survey methodology to findings, discussion, and recommendations.

Background
Why This Research Matters Here

Whatcom County sits at an interesting intersection — close proximity to tribal gaming facilities, a large college-age population, and the same saturation of mobile sports betting advertising that the rest of the country is navigating. This research asks a simple question: what is the local experience actually like?

The legalization of sports betting across the United States was made possible by the Supreme Court's 2018 decision to strike down the Professional and Amateur Sports Protection Act (PASPA). In the seven years since, 39 states have passed sports betting legislation, and the total volume of bets has grown almost 30 times over. Approximately 94% of those bets are now placed on a personal mobile device — meaning gambling has become private, portable, and always available.

This study was conducted to understand how that national story is playing out locally. A 12-question community survey was administered to Whatcom Community College students in March 2026, and the results were compared against five peer-reviewed academic sources covering advertising behavior, household financial impact, gambling culture, psychological harm, and mental health comorbidity.

Introduction
The Problem with Sports Betting Advertising

Advertising isn't incidental to gambling harm — research suggests it's built into how the industry works. The more exposure people have to betting ads, the more their gambling behavior changes. And the financial and mental health consequences extend well beyond the people who actually bet.

Gamblification: When Betting Becomes Normal

Researchers Hing, Rockloff, and Browne (2023) coined the term gamblification to describe the deliberate embedding of gambling culture into everyday sports fandom over the past 25 years. Betting operators sponsor professional teams, run commercials during live games, and push promotional offers through social media and mobile apps. Sports organizations are active participants in this — they derive revenue from wagering partnerships, which means their financial incentives are aligned with normalizing betting for their audiences.

"Novel betting products disproportionately extract revenues from vulnerable sports bettors and are therefore likely to exacerbate their gambling harm." — Hing, Rockloff & Browne (2023, p. 13)

The Dose-Response Effect

A 2025 systematic review of 22 quantitative studies by McGrane et al. found that sports-related gambling advertising is consistently associated with increases in gambling frequency, spending, and unplanned betting. But perhaps the most significant finding is the dose-response relationship — the harm from advertising isn't a simple on/off switch. The more advertising a person is exposed to, the stronger the effect on their gambling behavior becomes, similar to the way higher doses of a substance produce stronger effects.

"There is some evidence of a dose-response effect, whereby increased exposure to advertising is associated with an increasing effect on gambling behaviour." — McGrane et al. (2025, p. 2)

Financial Harm at the Household Level

A 2024 study from the National Bureau of Economic Research examined financial transaction data from more than 230,000 U.S. households across 26 states with legalized sports betting. The findings are striking: every dollar deposited into a sports betting app reduced investment contributions by just under a dollar. Credit card debt increased, available credit fell, and overdraft frequency rose — with effects concentrated in financially constrained households. The researchers offered a pointed conclusion about the likelihood of this resolving on its own:

"Because sports betting is addictive, these issues are unlikely to self correct." — Baker et al. (2024, p. 6)

Mental Health and Comorbidity

The mental health picture is equally serious. Sharma and Weinstein (2025) reviewed 49 studies on gambling disorder comorbidity and found that pathological gamblers showed 3.7 times higher risk of mood disorders and 3.1 times higher risk of anxiety disorders compared to non-gamblers. Critically, the relationship runs in both directions — in some cases gambling precedes the mental health condition, and in others the mental health condition comes first. This reciprocal relationship makes the problem particularly difficult to untangle clinically, and it means that community members dealing with anxiety or depression may be especially vulnerable to the normalized gambling environment that advertising creates.

"Pathological gamblers had a six-fold increased risk of a lifetime alcohol misuse diagnosis and a 4.4 times higher risk of a substance use disorder compared with non-gamblers. They were also about a 3 times higher probability of having major depression or dysthymia." — Sharma & Weinstein (2025, p. 3)
Research Methods
How This Research Was Conducted

This study combined secondary research synthesis with a primary community survey. Both phases were designed to generate findings that could be meaningfully compared against each other.

Secondary Research

Five peer-reviewed academic sources were identified and evaluated using the CRAAP framework (Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose). Sources were selected to cover advertising behavior, household financial impact, the cultural embedding of gambling, psychological distress, and mental health comorbidity — together forming a comprehensive picture of the problem at the national and international level.

Community Survey

A 12-question survey was distributed electronically via Google Forms to Whatcom Community College students in March 2026. Questions covered demographics, sports engagement, advertising exposure, betting behavior, and self-reported financial and mental health impact. Participation was voluntary and all responses were anonymous. 36 respondents consented and completed the survey.

Survey Population

58% of respondents were 21 or older; 42% were under 21. Income ranged from under $25,000 to over $150,000, with the largest group in the $25,000–$50,000 range. Most respondents engaged with sports at least occasionally — only the "never" option was unavailable given the wording — with 47% engaging weekly or daily.

Limitations

The survey population skews younger than the general Whatcom County population. This affects financial findings in particular — younger people and students have less financial exposure to gambling harm than working adults. Results should be read as a snapshot of college-age community members, not a representative sample of all Whatcom County residents.

Discussion
Local Findings vs. the National Picture

Where do Whatcom County results align with what the research predicts — and where do they diverge? The answer depends heavily on which question you're asking.

Where Results Line Up

The normalization finding is the clearest point of alignment. The literature is consistent that sports betting advertising is designed to make gambling seem ordinary and low-risk — and three quarters of Whatcom County respondents felt it was doing exactly that. This isn't surprising, but it's meaningful. Lopez-Gonzalez et al. (2025) establish that higher perceived impact of gambling advertising directly predicts gambling severity in clinical populations. The perception is the mechanism.

The mental health ripple effects also align well. A third of self-identified non-bettors still knew someone mentally affected by sports betting, which is consistent with Hing et al.'s finding that each problem gambler negatively affects an average of six people in their social network. The harm isn't contained to the people placing bets.

Where Results Diverge — and Why

The clearest divergence is on financial impact. Baker et al. (2024) found that financially constrained households in legalized betting states increased credit card balances by about $368 relative to less constrained households — an 8% increase in credit card debt, with overdraft frequency also rising. The local figure of 22% knowing someone financially affected is lower than what the national data would suggest for a comparable community survey.

The most likely explanation is age. Forty-two percent of local respondents were under 21, and most of the rest are college students who may not yet carry the financial responsibilities — mortgages, dependents, established savings — that make gambling harm most economically visible. Baker et al. were measuring household-level financial harm across working-age adults, which is a different population profile. The divergence here is probably a survey population artifact, not evidence that Whatcom County is financially insulated from sports betting harm.

The Comorbidity Angle

One of the more important frames for interpreting both datasets is the comorbidity relationship between problem gambling and mental illness. Sharma and Weinstein (2025) found that the relationship between gambling and mental health disorders is genuinely reciprocal — sometimes gambling causes the mental health condition, sometimes the mental health condition comes first and makes gambling more attractive as an escape. This means the 36% of local respondents who know someone mentally affected by sports betting may be observing more than just a correlation between troubled people and gambling. Some of those mental health problems may be gambling-induced. That's a stronger basis for community intervention than it might initially appear.

Summary & Recommendations
What the Research Suggests

Even in a small, college-oriented survey population, the normalization of sports betting advertising is clearly visible and widely perceived. The financial harm divergence doesn't mean the problem isn't here — it means we may not have found it yet with this particular sample.

  • 1
    ECPG: Prioritize college campus outreach. Forty-two percent of local respondents were under the legal betting age but already perceiving gambling as normalized. Early attitude formation matters. Reaching students before they can legally bet may be more effective than intervention after harm has occurred.
  • 2
    Opportunity Council: Include gambling in financial intake screening. Baker et al. (2024) show that financial harm from sports betting escalates over time and concentrates in constrained households. Clients experiencing housing instability or debt may be dealing with gambling-related financial strain even if they aren't presenting it as such.
  • 3
    Future researchers: Survey a broader adult population. The divergence in financial findings is most likely a sample artifact. A survey distributed through community partner organizations to working adults would likely produce results much closer to national data and give a fuller picture of harm in Whatcom County.
  • 4
    Advocacy: Use the 78% figure. Nearly four in five local respondents wanted harm-reduction information alongside betting advertisements. This is a local data point supporting what the ECPG already advocates for nationally — and it comes from a population that is mostly not betting themselves.
  • 5
    Future research: Examine the tribal casino context. Whatcom County's proximity to tribal gaming facilities is a meaningful variable this study wasn't equipped to capture. Research specifically examining the relationship between tribal casino proximity and mobile sports betting behavior could provide important regional context.
